Who Are We?

The African Church, also known as the "African Church Mission" or simply "The African Church," is one of the earliest African-initiated Christian denominations in Nigeria. It originated in the mid-19th century through the ministry of Samuel Ajayi Crowther, the first African Anglican bishop, and other African Christian leaders.

In 1862, a group of African Christians led by James Johnson established the "Native Church" in Lagos, marking the inception of the African-initiated church movement. Amidst disputes with European missionaries and the Church Missionary Society (CMS), the Native Church declared its independence in 1891 and adopted the name "The African Church" to emphasize its African identity and autonomy.

Despite facing opposition from European missionaries and colonial authorities, The African Church expanded its influence across Nigeria, particularly among the Yoruba-speaking population in southwestern Nigeria. James Johnson served as the first Superintendent of The African Church, followed by notable leaders such as Jacob Kehinde Coker and Joseph Olu Omolaja Adetiloye. The African Church blends Anglicanism with indigenous African religious practices and cultural traditions, emphasizing African spirituality, autonomy, and self-governance within a Christian framework.

It operates under a hierarchical structure with a Superintendent-General overseeing the denomination, organized into dioceses, districts, and local congregations. The African Church is actively engaged in evangelism, education, healthcare, and social welfare programs, running schools, hospitals, and other institutions to serve its members and communities. Throughout its history, The African Church has played a significant role in promoting indigenous leadership, cultural identity, and religious autonomy among African Christians in Nigeria.
